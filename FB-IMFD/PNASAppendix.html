1.Introduction

This report is a supplementary material of the paper  ““Will we ever understand Facebook's influence on elections? A scientists’ perspective””.

This document describes our research questions, the Facebook URL Dataset on which our experiments were performed and our results. As described in the main paper, our main goal was to study the effect and sharing patterns of rumors and fake news on Facebook in Latin America countries.

The Facebook URL Dataset is a closed-access dataset about shared URLs on the Facebook platform. The dataset includes view, share, and click counts; counts of reactions to the posts that share them (such as likes, angers, etc.); demographic data about the users (age range, gender, etc.); and information about the URLs themselves (country where they were shared the most, title, description, etc). In order to protect user privacy, the dataset was postprocessed by applying privacy-protection methods, such as differential privacy, suppression, and generalization of attributes.

We define two categories of URLs as our objects of study:

  • Category 1: URLs pointing to content generated by professional, reputable news media. We also refer to this category as “main” throughout this document.
  • Category 2: URLs pointing to content generated by sources previously known for publishing fake news or debunked rumors. We also refer to this category as “fake” throughout this document.

Our experiments attempted to find differences between these two categories, in particular, during the latest presidential election periods of two latin american countries, Chile and Colombia.

The remainder of this paper is structured as follows. Section 2 describes our research questions. Section 3 presents an exploratory analysis of our dataset. Section 4 gives an overview of our methods and experiments. Section 5 presents all our results. Finally, Section 6 summarizes all results in a table.

2. Research Questions

This project aimed to answer the following research questions for our two target countries, Chile and Colombia.

Q1. Are there significant differences between URLs (in terms of number of views and reactions) from professional news media (Category 1) and URLs from sources that are known for publishing fake news in both Chile and Colombia (Category 2)?

Q2. Which features of a URL present significant differences for these two categories for both Chile and Colombia?

Q3. Does the election period (pre-election or post-election) in which the URL was created affect these differences for both Chile and Colombia?

3. Dataset description

The Facebook URL Dataset describes data about URLs shared on Facebook, and actions performed on posts that share those URLs by users of the platform. The dataset consists of two tables: URL Attributes and URL Breakdowns.

The URL Attributes table contains information about the URLs themselves. It has attributes such as the “sanitized” URL (after removing possibly identifying information), the date and time of the first post that shared it on Facebook, the amount of spam, hate speech, and false news flag reports, and the country where it was shared most times in the span of the dataset. For a URL to be included in this dataset, it had to be shared publicly by at least one hundred users (plus Laplacian-distributed noise). The table contains about 38 million URLs from 46 countries, spanning from January 2017 to August, 2019.

The URL Breakdowns table contains information about actions performed by groups of users to posts containing URLs in Facebook. A group of users is defined by a tuple (year, month, country, age bracket, gender), meaning that a row of this table corresponds to the actions given to a specific URL by a group of users of a certain country, age bracket, gender, during a specific year-month. There are 31 year-months, 3 genders, and 7 age brackets. There also is a political affinity computed column for U.S. users only, with 5 possible values. This gives about 767 billion rows in total.

The actions described in the URL Breakdowns table are the following: post views, post shares, the amount of shares without clicking the link, the amount of comments, the amount of clicks on the URL, and reactions to the posts, such as “likes,” “loves,” “hahas,” “sorrys,” “angers,” and “wows.”

All numeric attributes, such as the number of shares or hate speech reports, have noise added in order to satisfy the differential privacy measures imposed by Facebook. This added noise induces measurement error to each of these values. To operationalize this requirement, zero-mean normally distributed noise was added to each value independently (see Table below).

We first began by defining groups of interest for our analysis. As the dataset contains metadata about the URLs, such as the date of first publication on Facebook, the country where they were shared most times, or the parent domain (e.g., “cnn.com” is the parent domain of “edition.cnn.com/world”), we used some of those attributes to match external URLs based on external criteria.

In our case, we defined groups of URLs based on whether the URL was first published before or after election day in a given country (pre-election, post-election), and whether it came from a domain known to have published misinformative (disinformation?) articles in the past (disreputable domain), or from traditional or mainstream media (mainstream domain). 4. Methods and Experiments

To answer our questions, we conducted two types of analyses:

  1. Descriptive analysis: we use descriptive methods, such as descriptive statistics, visualization, and prototype vector representation of the data.
  2. Predictive analysis: we train statistical or machine learning models on the data and assess the predictive capability of these models between different groups of interest.

These analyses were conducted by performing the following experiments:

E1. Calculating summary statistics of all variables for the two groups in both periods. (Descriptive)

E2. Data visualization techniques for comparing prototype multi-dimensional vectors of the URLs from these two groups. (Descriptive)

E3. Conducted Mood's median tests for each URL variable using the Bonferroni correction for multiple comparison. The null hypothesis here is that the median of each variable is equal for both groups. (Descriptive)

E4. Trained classification models (i.e., decision trees) using independent training and testing partitions for classifying a URL into a category. (Predictive)

E5. Used the Boruta feature selection technique to identify relevant variables for classifying a URL into a category. (Predictive)

E6.  We used PrivacyUnbiased and Facebook’s svinfer libraries to fit linear regression models using the category as a binary response variable. In this library the estimators are corrected according to the DP-noise of each variable. (Predictive)

5. Experiments and Results

E1. Calculating summary statistics

We can expect the random noise to cancel itself when computing the mean of a sample with sufficient number of observations. Recall that each value of a given numeric attribute in the dataset has been added with an independent zero-mean, normally distributed value (also called classical measurement error).

We compared the averages between attributes and groups and observed that in both countries –Chile and Colombia–, URLs shared post-election had less views and clicks than in the pre-election period.

In order to have a reasonable comparison between fake and mainstream domains, the need of a third category, called the “Regular domains” arises.

The idea is to generate a sample similar to fake and mainstream domains, at least according to the number of URLs per domain in the sample.

In order to achieve this, we filtered the Breakdowns table  to consider URLs corresponding to the top 100 domains in terms of number of URLs per domain that were published during the pre and post election periods. More precisely,  the “regular” domain category was sampled from the Regular domains using a matching strategy: For each domain in the Fake sample, find the 3 nearest neighbours from the Regular domains using views, clicks and shares as features.

The procedure to get the corresponding breakdowns is as follows:

Given configuration (country=COLOMBIA, period=POST_ELECTION_DATE_RANGE):

  • Filter rows from AT such that:
  • public_shares_top_country is COLOMBIA
  • date(first_post_time) is within POST_ELECTION_DATE_RANGE
  • The output is a set of tuples (url_rid, parent_domain)
  • Filter rows from BT such that:
  • url_rid is contained in the list obtained from AT above
  • year_month covers the period POST_ELECTION_DATE_RANGE
  • country is COLOMBIA
  • The output is a set of tuples (url_rid, age_bracket, gender, year_month, views, clicks, shares, likes, loves, hahas, wows, sorrys, angers, comments, share_without_clicks)

Likewise with the previous data extraction, this is analogous to other configurations (country, period). In this case, the “domains” parameter is fixed at “regular” (along with “fake” and “mainstream” in the previous section).

The main findings of this analysis for the case of Chile can be summarized as follows:

  • We observed differences between the two categories in almost all the features, except loves, share_without_clicks, and shares.
  • The features with highest differences in the two categories were sorrys (7 times more in Category 1 than 2), wows (3.7 times), views (3.1 times), clicks (2.2 times), hahas (1.9 times), and likes (1.8 times).
  • By comparing features in the same category in different periods, we observed the following differences.
  • Category 1: sorrys (2.1 times more in the post-election period), angers (1.6 times), wows (1.3 times).
  • Category 2: hahas (2.4 times more in the post-election period), share_without_clicks (1.6 times), shares (1.5 times)

And for the case of Colombia:

  • We observed differences between the two categories in all the features.
  • The features with highest differences in the two categories were views (7.8 times more in category 1), sorrys (7.1 times), hahas (5.2 times) clicks (4.7 times), and likes (3.9 times)
  • By comparing features in the same category in different periods, we observed the following differences.
  • Category 1: sorrys (1.5 times more in post-election), angers (1.2 times), and views (1.2 times more in the pre-election period).
  • Category 2: views (1.4 times more in pre-election), clicks (1.3 times more in pre-election), and sorrys (1.2 times in post-election)

E2. Data visualization techniques for comparing prototype multi-dimensional vectors

Another way to understand the difference between the groups of interest is to measure the change in the amount of user actions (view, click, like, etc.) w.r.t. a domain from pre-election period to post-election period. For instance, to understand whether a news source known to have published unreliable or misinformative content has received more or less views after election day. In this analysis we considered a URL domain (a news source) as the unit of analysis in order to have a large enough sample to cancel out the noise. We represented a domain as a vector of the mean of selected attributes, in this case views, clicks, and shares, and observed the change in euclidean distance between the vector computed from data pre-election and post-election.

The following figures show the vectors per domain/period/country with 95% confidence intervals (blue error bars).

Prototype multi-dimensional vectors.

The following figure shows domains according to the amount of URLs they have (x-axis) versus the Euclidean distance between their vectors (y-axis), disaggregated by country. We observe that some “fake” domains have the largest change between pre and post-election period. However, the amount of URLs in those domains is very low (17 URLs in the case of ‘t13chile.cf’ versus ~6000 in ‘cooperativa.cl’) and it is not possible to observe notable differences between election periods.

From the figure we can conclude that distance between domains from one period to the other shows some “distant” domains in category 1. However, this could be explained due to very few URLs in those domains. This applies to both Chile and Colombia.

E3. Hypothesis tests to compare medians

We then wanted to observe if those differences were statistically significant. We chose a non-parametric test as we do not know the underlying distribution of the data behind the noise. Additionally, user actions on digital content often form skewed distributions (citation needed) and we believe the median of the distribution could be a better option in order to compare two groups. In this case, we conducted Mood’s median hypothesis test to compare two groups (e.g., median likes of URLs from disreputable sources posted on pre-election period versus post-election period), and applied Bonferroni correction on the p-values in order to avoid Type I errors. We found some statistically significant differences between medians, and commonalities across the two studied countries.

However, the differences are not the same between the two countries. For instance, for views and clicks there are significant differences between their medians, for both countries, but differences in shares and share_without_clicks are significant for Colombia and not for Chile, and vice versa.

This means that the added noise could affect the results depending on the underlying distribution of the data.

E4. Classification models

We conducted a predictive analysis in order to determine if there is a signal in the data that would allow us to distinguish between groups of URLs. For instance, if it is possible to train a classifier with reasonable predictive performance in order to tell apart URLs from disreputable sources and mainstream sources, then it would suggest that there is a strong signal in the data despite the noise. We trained decision trees using an independent test set, and we found that the views attribute is the most useful to distinguish URLs from disreputable and mainstream domains. This is not surprising given the “by definition” popularity aspect of mainstream domains.

The main results and findings from this experiment for the case of Chile are summarized as follows:

  • Being category 2 the target class, we observed acceptable prediction scores: a F1 score of 0.55, and a Cohen’s Kappa value of 0.50 between the real labels.
  • The most relevant features to the model predicting the category, given a URL with aggregated features (by demographic values) were clicks, views, comments, likes, and shares. When removing the ‘views’ feature, the classifier assigns everything to the largest class (category 1).
  • When predicting the period, the most relevant features were clicks, shares, views, likes, and share_without_clicks.
  • Restricted to category 1, and the pre-election period being the target class, we observed a F1 score of 0.67, and a Cohen’s Kappa value of 0.14.
  • Restricted to category 2, and the post-election period being the target class, we observed a F1 score of 0.70 and a Cohen’s Kappa value of 0.02.

And for the case of Colombia:

  • Being category 2 the target class, we observed a F1 score of 0.59, and a Cohen’s Kappa value of 0.57 between the real labels.
  • The most relevant features to the model predicting the category, given a URL with aggregated features (by demographic values) were views, comments, shares, clicks, and  share_without_clicks. When removing the ‘views’ feature, the model assigns every URL to the largest class (category 1).
  • When predicting the period, the most relevant features were  share_without_clicks, shares, views, likes, and clicks.
  • Restricted to category 1, and the pre-election period being the target class, we observed a F1 score of 0.55, and a Cohen’s Kappa value of 0.16.
  • Restricted to category 2, and the post-election period being the target class, we observed a F1 score of 0.50 and a Cohen’s Kappa value of -0.00.

Complementary to the decision tree approach, we also performed a feature selection step in order to identify the most salient features in the data. E6.  PrivacyUnbiased Linear regression

Finally, we performed linear regression experiments using an adapted method to overcome the known measurement error given by the amount of noise added to the data. We used PrivacyUnbiased (https://github.com/georgieevans/PrivacyUnbiased) and the svinfer library provided by Facebook.

A major shortcoming of this library is a potential mismatch between the nature of our problem and the method employed: our problem is a classification task with a binary dependent variable, and PrivacyUnbiased is designed to work with continuous response variables. We decided to treat the PrivacyUnbiased regression model as a linear probability model, which is the standard interpretation of applying linear regression models to binary classification tasks (a logistic regression is a more appropriate choice, but a DP variant of this model has not been developed as far as we know).
